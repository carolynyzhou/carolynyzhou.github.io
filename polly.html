Carolyn Zhou

POLLY

Challenge

To equip Low English Proficiency (LEP) patients and their caretakers with the information and resources to request quality translation services in healthcare settings.

Outcome

Our product, POLLY, facilitates communication between LEP patients and their doctors by providing training and services to those acting as non-professional translators.

Role Researcher, Designer

Team Lily Kim (designer), Helen Kim (developer), Sophie Zhao (developer)

Overview

Inaccuracies in medical translation can lead to misinformed, dangerous decisions about a patient's health. Despite this risk, patients often ask unqualified family members or caretakers to fill in as "ad hoc" translators instead of a professional. Through our research, we identified factors that contribute to this problen and sought to alleviate them for the ad hoc translator.

We designed 2 touchpoints. The first is a pocket course that provides users with professional tips for medical interpretation. It would be found in waiting rooms or doctor’s offices to introduce users to POLLY and direct them to the mobile app. The app offers POLLY's full services, allowing users to send requests for professional translators, translate more effectively, and track the patient's medical activity.

Users

Profile

  • Age: 15-35
  • Biracial or non-Caucasian
  • Child or relative of an immigrant

User Needs

  • To be informed about language services in health care
  • Training to effectively help translate
  • Better communication with doctors and with the patient
  • Doctor-patient confidentiality

Goals & Challenges

Our target users are family members of LEP patients who step in as "ad hoc" translators during doctor's appointments. Typically English is still their primary language, so they may feel frustrated or ill-equipped to translate technical terminology. They want better options, or relief from the role.

Research

Ad Hoc Interviews

We found that second generation children often translated for older family members. As immigrants, many LEP patients also carry “at-risk” status (uninsured, undocumented, or of low-SES background), making medical visits even more stressful. Further, many had never even been offered proper translation services during visits. The family often wasn't aware of their options, or if they were they didn't feel comfortable with having a stranger translate.

My parents thought I was more legit [than a professional translator]. And they trust me; if I mess up, they know it wasn't on purpose.

Expert Interviews

We saw great diversity in how health centers handled medical translation, depending on the community surrounding them. Doctors working in medically underserved areas seemed more knowledgeable about immigrant needs. Additionally, some clinics were much stricter than others on consulting professional translators and had more efficient systems to reach them.

Family members will put their own spin on things. If they don't understand what the physician is saying, they'll skip it. We use family as a last resort.

Research Analysis

We identified common points of stress for caretakers before, during, and after most appointments: when setting appointments, when translating, and when retrieving the patient’s treatment or following up with a specialist. This process helped us identify scenarios into which we might introduce a design intervention.

Development

Pocket Course Development

With our research in mind, we decided one of the best things we could do was to keep ad hoc translators well-informed about their rights to quality care and a professional translator, as well as best practices should they find themselves in a translator role.

We crafted a “crash course” filled with interpretation tips from professionals, designed to feel reassuring and fit in the user’s pocket. Our development process involved test prints, paper prototypes, and drafting content.

App Development

The app’s purpose was to offer users more abundant resources in digital form. As one of 2 designers I conducted research to determine helpful app features and to test usability, created wireframes and user flow, wrote copy, and created assets.

Final Deliverables

Pocket Course

The pocket course provides professional translation tips, bringing attention to common errors. We prototyped in different languages to demonstrate how we might consider linguistic design nuances.

Mobile App

The mobile app supports users in requesting professional translation services, providing translation resources, and managing patient information.

Learning Outcomes

  • Identifying a problem and its audience, constraints, and physical context through user research
  • Researching a systemic and sensitive problem space
  • Navigating issues of trust and confidentiality, as well as policies and regulations, when designing user flows
  • Considering nuances for diverse and localized needs (i.e. text in different languages, use of imagery in other cultural contexts, distribution methods)
  • Creating a system with multiple touchpoints and carrying a consistent visual voice

Reflection

If we had time to develop POLLY further, I would love to investigate partnerships with existing medical translation services and Pittsburgh's healthcare infrastructure. It would be interesting to learn how to culminate relationships with health and relocation centers to build a trusted network of resources for LEP clients.
