Carolyn Zhou

POLLY

Challenge

To equip Low English Proficiency (LEP) patients and their caretakers with the information and resources to request quality healthcare.

Outcome

Our product, POLLY, facilitates communication between LEP patients and their doctors by providing training and services to those acting as non-professional translators.

Role

  • Designer
  • Researcher

Collaborators

  • Lily Kim (designer)
  • Helen Kim (developer)
  • Sophie Zhao (developer)

Overview & Deliverables

Background

Inaccuracies in medical translation can lead to misinformed, dangerous decisions about a patient's health. Despite this risk, patients often ask unqualified, bilingual caretakers to fill in as "ad hoc" translators instead of a professional. We aimed to alleviate the factors contributing to this problem, ranging from discomfort to lack of awareness.

Pocket Course

The pocket course is designed to provide ad hoc translators with professional training by bringing attention to common errors and recommending best practices in medical translation. It would be found in waiting rooms or doctor’s offices to introduce users to POLLY and direct them to the mobile app.

Mobile App

The mobile app offers POLLY's full services. The app aims to support ad hoc translators in their primary role as caretakers, enabling them to send requests for professional translators, translate more effectively, track the patient's medical activity, and more.

Research

Ad Hoc Interview Insights

We reached out to people who have acted as non-professional translators (our target users) for interviews. We found that second generation children of immigrant parents often translated for older family members. As immigrants, many LEP patients also carry “at-risk” status (uninsured, undocumented, or of low-SES background), making medical visits even more stressful. Further, many federally funded centers do not offer professional translation services without prompting, as the laws requiring this action are not enforced.

My parents thought I was more legit [than a professional translator]. And they trust me; if I mess up, they know it wasn't on purpose.

Expert Interview Insights

We saw great diversity in how health centers handled medical translation, depending on the communities surrounding them. Doctors working in medically underserved areas seemed more knowledgeable about immigrant needs and were better equipped to serve them. Additionally, some clinics were much stricter than others on consulting professional translators and had more efficient systems to reach them.

Family members will put their own spin on things. If they don't understand what the physician is saying, they'll skip it. We use family as a last resort.

Research Analysis

We identified common points of stress for caretakers before, during, and after most appointments: when setting appointments, when translating, and when retrieving the patient’s treatment or following up with a specialist. This process helped us identify scenarios into which we might introduce a design intervention.

User Needs

Through our research, we found 4 user needs emphasized by medical professionals, caretakers, and translators:

  • The ability to make informed decisions about language services in health care
  • Training to minimize common translation errors
  • Better communication with doctors and with the patient
  • Respect for doctor-patient confidentiality

Development

Pocket Course Development

With our research in mind, we decided one of the best things we could do was to keep ad hoc translators well-informed about their options, their right to request a translator, and best practices should they find themselves in a translator role.

We crafted a “crash course” filled with translation tips from professionals, designed to feel reassuring and fit in the user’s pocket. Our development process involved test prints, paper prototypes, and drafting content.

App Development

The app’s purpose was to offer users more abundant resources in digital form. As one of 2 designers I conducted research to determine helpful app features and to test usability, created wireframes and prototypes, wrote copy, and created assets.

Learning Outcomes

  • Identifying a problem and its audience, constraints, and physical context through user research
  • Researching a systemic and sensitive problem space
  • Navigating issues of trust and confidentiality, as well as policies and regulations, when designing user flows
  • Considering nuances for diverse and localized needs (i.e. text in different languages, use of imagery in other cultural contexts, distribution methods)
  • Creating a system with multiple touchpoints and carrying a consistent visual voice

Reflection

If we had time to develop POLLY further, I would love to investigate partnerships with existing medical translation services and Pittsburgh's healthcare infrastructure. It would be interesting to learn how to culminate relationships with health and relocation centers to build a trusted network of resources for LEP clients.
