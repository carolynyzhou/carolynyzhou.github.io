Easing ad hoc medical translation

Challenge

To equip and empower caretakers of non-English speaking patients to acquire quality medical translation services.

Outcome

A mobile app and print piece for POLLY, our service that facilitates communication between LEP patients, doctors and caretakers.

Role Researcher & UX designer

Team Lily Kim (designer), Helen Kim (developer), Sophie Zhao (developer)

Overview

From personal experiences, my team found a need to address the emotional and cultural barriers preventing proper communication between American doctors and low-English proficiency patients, who often rely on unqualified friends or family to be "ad hoc" translators, in place of a professional.

We designed 2 touchpoints:

1) The pocket course provides ad hocs with quick professional tips for medical interpretation. Found in waiting rooms or doctor’s offices, this piece also directs them over to the mobile app.

2) The mobile app offers POLLY's full services, allowing users to make requests for professional translators, translate more effectively, and track the patient's medical activity.

Research

Validation

We began with background research to gauge the scale of this problem: how many people it affected, and to what extent.

Over 60 million people in the US are bilingual, and over 40% of them are considered Low English Proficient (LEP).

We guessed that if most people had family translate their appointments, like we'd seen, then many weren't getting reliable information and follow-up. The long-term effects of this oversight include unfilled prescriptions, the endangered health of millions, and inflated costs for delayed treatment.

Ad Hoc Interviews

Speaking with 10 people (interviews conducted by our team in pairs) who have acted as non-official translators, we learned more about the factors that cause them stress during medical visits.

  • Second generation children often translate for older family members.
  • As immigrants, many LEP patients carry “at-risk” status (uninsured, undocumented, of low-SES background).
  • Most families were never aware of formal translation services.
  • Many patients don't feel comfortable with a stranger in the room during medical visits anyway.
My parents think I'm more legit than [a professional translator]. And they trust me; if I mess up, it wasn't on purpose.

Expert Interviews

Conversely, we found differing viewpoints when speaking with medical experts. We visited 3 different hospitals/health clinics around Pittsburgh.

  • Some clinics are much stricter than others on consulting professional translators only. They tend to have more efficient systems to reach them.
  • Health centers handle medical translation differently depending on the community around them. For example, in areas with high immigrant populations, doctors ask locals to translate.
Family members put their own spin on things. If they don't understand what the doctor is saying, they skip it. We use family as a last resort.

Users & Scenarios

Profile

  • Age: 15-35
  • Biracial or non-Caucasian
  • Child or relative of an immigrant

User Needs

  • More effective translation tools or options
  • Respect for doctor-patient confidentiality

Our users are family members of non-native speakers who act as translators in the patient's appointments. Typically English is still their primary language, so they feel frustrated and ill-equipped to translate medical terminology.

Stakeholder Map

We identified the ad hoc translator as our target user because of their unique position in the patient/doctor dynamic. Aside from translation, they provide emotional support and ensure trust for the patient in a way professionals cannot replicate.

Research Analysis

From our research, we identified common points of stress for caretakers before, during, and after appointments, including scheduling, translating, retrieving prescriptions and following up with specialists.

Development

Pocket Course Development

With these takeaways in mind, we decided our design should keep ad hoc translators well-informed about their rights to quality care and a professional translator, as well as best practices for translation.

We crafted a “crash course” filled with interpretation tips from professionals, designed to feel reassuring and fit in the user’s pocket.

App Development

The app’s purpose was to offer users more abundant resources in digital form. I determined useful features through research, created wireframes and user flows, wrote copy, tested usability and created assets.

Final Deliverables

Pocket Course

The pocket course provides professional translation tips, bringing attention to common errors. We prototyped in different languages to demonstrate how we might consider linguistic design nuances.

Mobile App

The mobile app supports users in requesting professional translation services, providing translation resources, and managing patient information.

Requesting a Translator

Our primary focus, this feature allows patients to request a translator they would be comfortable with ahead of time. Due to doctor's preferences, and overall better quality of translation, we wanted this option to be the patient's first choice as well.

Viewing Patient Profiles

Because of other tasks that may fall to the caretaker, like picking up prescriptions or catching up various doctors on the patient's situation, we created a place for them to track information. We also built in some measures for patient privacy.

Resources

In cases where the patient is unable to get a professional translator, we wanted to ensure that the ad hoc is at least well-equipped for the task. The Resources section includes a dictionary for medical terms, and a digital Pocket Course.

Desired Results

  • An increase in number of immigrant families coming in for regular medical checkups
  • An increase in usage of medical translation services, and more efficient systems implemented
  • Fewer unfilled prescriptions

Learning Outcomes

  • Researching a systemic and sensitive problem space
  • Navigating issues of trust and confidentiality, as well as policies and regulations
  • Considering nuances for diverse and localized needs (i.e. text in different languages, use of imagery in other cultural contexts, distribution methods)
  • Carrying a consistent visual voice across multiple touchpoints
